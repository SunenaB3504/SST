Were Lal-Bal-Pal in favour of strikes and boycotts? The partition of Bengal The British were unhappy at the growing popularity of the Congress. They thought of a plan to prevent Hindus and Muslims from uniting against them. In 1905, they divided the province of Bengal into two. One part was to have largely Hindus while the other half was to have mostly Muslims. The government said that Bengal was a large area and they were dividing it only to make administration easier. But the people knew the real motive and decided to protest against the partition. The Swadeshi-Boycott Movement The partition angered the people. The Congress leaders decided to start a mass movement. They asked people to stop using (boycott) goods made in England. Instead, people were asked to use ‘swadeshi or goods made in India. Foreign clothes were burnt in huge bonfires. A large number of students and women joined the movement. The government arrested thousands of people and put them in prison. The protests against the Bengal partition continued for many years. The British finally accepted defeat. In 1911, they reunited Bengal. It was a victory for the people of India. The whole of India celebrated.       

     Jana Gana Mana was composed by ‘Gurudev’ Rabindranath Tagore. It was sung for the first time at the Calcutta session of the Indian National Congress in 1911. It was adopted as India’s National Anthem in 1950. The early revolutionaries Many young people were not happy with the methods of protest adopted by the Congress leaders. They believed that the British could be driven out of India only through force. Most of these early revolutionaries were from Bengal, Punjab and Maharashtra. The Chapekar brothers, V. D. Savarkar, Prafulla Chandra Chaki and Khudiram Bose were some brave Words to know   revolutionaries of this period. They gave up their lives for their motherland. The arrival of Gandhiji In 1915, Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi returned to India after spending many years in South Africa. He started the next phase of the freedom movement. It would end in 1947, with India attaining freedom.
